“When you hear the word "deadline,"
what thought comes to mind?”

In my senior year of high school, I signed up for Journalism.

The class put out a paper on a monthly basis--a small order, realistically. To add to the simplicity, there was a series of well-spaced deadlines along the way to make sure everyone had plenty of time to write, edit, and submit the content for placement.

According to the schedule, we'd sign up for one or two articles the first week, work on them for a couple more weeks, and then we'd submit content to the editors for placement at the end of the month so that the files could be sent to the printer that worked with the school.

It was a simple process that gave everyone lots of time to do what they needed, but that was definitively not how the class worked out.

Most signed up on time, but few finished with similar punctuality. Most turned their articles in past the submission deadline, and some even wrote theirs after the day of the deadline.

Most of the class didn't seem to know or care that there was a final, undeclared deadline every month--the printer's deadline. If we didn’t sign up on time according to the schedule it was inconsequential, but if we missed that final deadline, an entire issue would never make it to print.

Every single month, toward that last, looming date, I would stay after school (past midnight on some occasions) performing "editorial duties" with the handful of other dedicated student editors. In description, our job was to place our classmates' content into our layouts.

In practice, it was frantic, caffeine-fueled madness. We'd most often spend the time tweaking to our layouts and typing up fluff articles to compensate for all the empty space so we could turn something in to the printers' the following morning.

Despite the pressure and time commitment, work nights were a blast. The class itself never taught me much about journalism, but those monthly production crams certainly taught me things that one just can't pick up from a packet or PowerPoint.

They taught me "soft skills" like time management, common-sense design practices, and effective collaboration with others as well as a set of hard skills--the ability to work in Creative Suite programs like InDesign and Photoshop.

I'm a good-humored, quiet kid from a working-class family that was very poor for a very long time. I also don't want to have to choose what I study with making money in mind--I want to learn for learning's sake.

My original plan was to hit community college, then transfer to a four-year. The experience was labor-of-Sisyphus-tier absurd. I was going into debt to buy books I would never open for classes I was only taking because I was unable to take the classes I actually wanted, ones I needed to transfer out in two years.

I dropped out partway into my second semester. I wasn't failing or anything, it just felt drawn-out and directionless. I didn't know what I wanted to do and didn't identify with the experience.

With my family's support, I've had the opportunity to set my priorities straight, and have decided to pursue a career in design and development. It'd really be more than a career, though, since it's something I feel strongly about and would like to do every day for the rest of my life.

I love web design, and have taught myself to code in several languages to accommodate my vision. This site isn't a tumblr or a wordpress. I hand-coded it two days ago and edited the text last night.
